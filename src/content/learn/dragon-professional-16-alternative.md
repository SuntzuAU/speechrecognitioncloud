If you're weighing up Dragon Professional 16, you're probably in one of two camps. Either you already run it and it has stopped feeling worth the money — or you're on Dragon 15, you've been told it won't run properly on Windows 11, and Nuance's answer is to buy Professional 16. Either way, you're asking the same question: is paying (again) for Dragon really the best move in 2026?

You don't need another sales pitch — you've had enough of those. You need to know whether there's genuinely something better, and what the honest trade-offs are. So here's the short answer first, then the reasons behind it.

The best alternative to Dragon Professional 16 is **Speech Recognition Cloud (SRC)**. It does the one thing you actually want — put your words on the screen, in any Windows application, right at the cursor — without the cost, the install limits, the compatibility headaches, or the training. Below is exactly how it stacks up on the five things Dragon users tell us frustrate them most, worst first.

## 1. The cost

This is the one almost everyone raises. Dragon Professional 16 is a large one-off purchase — several hundred dollars — and that's before you count paying again for the next upgrade. At some point the maths simply stops making sense.

**How SRC compares:** SRC is $99/year for the Personal plan and $159/year for Professional — both with *unlimited* dictation. There's also a genuinely free **Starter** plan: 20 minutes of dictation a month, core features, no credit card, and no trial clock counting down. It just stays free. That means you can try the real product — not a locked-down demo — before you spend a cent. (Prices are shown in US dollars and convert to your local currency at checkout; Australian customers see an AUD price with GST included.)

## 2. The two-computer install limit

This is the pain Dragon users raise with the most heat, and fairly so. Dragon's standard licence restricts how many machines you can install on. Hit that limit and you're stuck — deactivating one PC, calling support, or buying another licence just to run your own software on your own laptop *and* your own desktop.

**How SRC compares:** SRC is licensed **per person, not per machine**. One licence, installed on as many computers as you like — desktop, laptop, the PC at the clinic, the one at home. It's your licence; use it wherever you are. No install counting, no deactivation dance, no second purchase to work in two places.

## 3. Windows 11 — and the upgrade you're being pushed into

Here's the situation a lot of people are actually in: **Nuance itself states that Dragon 15 is not compatible with Windows 11.** It often runs — but with the lag, freezing and glitches widely documented by users pushed onto Windows 11 by a new PC or an IT policy. The fix Nuance offers is to buy Dragon Professional 16 — which lands you straight in the next problem: a fresh several-hundred-dollar outlay for a release that, as we'll get to, adds very little over the version you already own.

**How SRC compares:** SRC is cloud-based, so there's no local "version" to fall out of step with your operating system. The heavy lifting happens in the cloud, and updates roll out automatically. It works on current Windows and keeps working as Windows updates — nothing to reinstall, no paid upgrade to chase compatibility, nothing to fall behind.

## 4. Paying to upgrade — for barely any change

Part of what stings is how little the upgrade actually buys you. Users and reviewers have long noted that recent Dragon releases differ little from the ones before — so moving from Dragon 15 to Professional 16 can feel like paying premium money largely to stand still (and to win back the Windows 11 support you used to have). Then the same decision comes around again at the next version.

**How SRC compares:** SRC is a subscription that's **continuously updated**. New features, better accuracy and more languages arrive automatically as part of what you already pay. There's no "buy the new version to get the improvements" — the SRC you use in twelve months will be better than today's, at no extra cost, and there's no upgrade purchase looming.

## 5. Voice training you shouldn't need anymore

Older dictation software made you read scripts aloud to "train" it before it would understand you — then re-train it when it drifted. In 2026, that's an anachronism.

**How SRC compares:** SRC needs **no voice training**. It uses modern AI speech recognition, so you open it and start talking — cold, first time, no enrolment, no reading passages into a microphone. Setup takes about two minutes, and then you're dictating.

## Is cloud dictation private?

A fair question — especially in medical, legal and government work. Here's the honest architecture: your audio is processed in memory and erased immediately, your transcribed text is not stored on SRC's servers, and your data is never used to train AI models. We don't claim SRC is "HIPAA compliant" as a product, because compliance is a property of a *deployment*, not a piece of software — a distinction any serious vendor should make. You can read the full detail on our [privacy and security page](/privacy).

## What about working offline?

Being straight with you: because SRC is cloud-based, it needs an internet connection. That's the trade for always-current accuracy, no local training, and never falling out of step with Windows. If you dictate somewhere with no connectivity, that's worth weighing — for almost everyone working at a desk or in a clinic, it's a non-issue.
